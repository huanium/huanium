Research Experience & Interests

Atomic, Molecular, and Optical Physics
I've been working with professor Charles Conover at Colby College since my freshman spring. The ongoing project at the time I joined the lab was measuring energy spacings between Rydberg states of ultracold potassium atoms to high precision (to a part in 10 million). Ultracold K atoms are prepared in a magneto-optical trap (MOT) and laser-cooled to around 1 mK. We are hoping to get some collision experiments going soon.

Experimental: Conover lab
My first lab experience was constructing a laser frequency locking circuit, which takes in a reference signal from the photodiodes and an "error" signal, and produces a feedback signal that keeps the laser wavelength at fixed value.

I worked on a number of small projects over the summer of 2018, but spent a big chunk in the end learning how we could apply Ramsey's separated oscillatory fields method with our current set-up to perform high-precision spectroscopy.

Summer 2018: Ramsey's Separated Oscillating Field Method
I spent most of my summer research at Conover's lab studying how to use Ramsey's oscillating field method to make high-precision measurements without the need for AC Stark shift elimination. We already had a functioning system for high-precision spectroscopy: (i) Rydberg excitation, (ii) DC-Stark shift elimination, (iii) AC-Stark shift elimination. So what left was replacing the previous single-pulse Rydberg excitation with a double-pulse Rydberg excitation (which produced Ramsey fringes), and removing step (iii). The most fun part was deriving (from Ramsey's 1950 paper) a model for fitting the data to find the desired (with zero AC-Startk shift) transition frequency.

I presented this work at Colby Undergraduate Summer Research Retreat 2018 (CUSRR 2018). Check out my poster here.

The derivation for the model can be found here. I reproduced this derivation from the handwritten version here which I made over summer.

Want to get interactive? Download and run this simulation in Mathematica and play around with the Rabi rate, $\Omega$, delay time, $t$, and pulse width, $\tau$. See how the population of the excited state (square amplitude of the excited state's wavefunction) changes as a function of detuning $\delta$.

Basically, the population of atoms in the excited state can be modelled by this equation:

$$P_2 = 4\sin^{2}\theta\sin^{2}\frac{\Omega'\tau}{2} \left\lbrace \cos \frac{\Omega'\tau}{2}\cos \frac{\Delta_0 T}{2} - \cos\theta\sin\frac{\Omega'\tau}{2} \sin \frac{\Delta_0 T}{2}\right\rbrace$$

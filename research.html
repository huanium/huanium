Research Experience & Interests

Atomic, Molecular, and Optical Physics
I've been working with professor Charles Conover at Colby College since my freshman spring. The ongoing project at the time I joined the lab was measuring energy spacings between Rydberg states of ultracold potassium atoms to high precision (to a part in 10 million). Ultracold K atoms are prepared in a magneto-optical trap (MOT) and laser-cooled to around 1 mK. More details coming soon

Theoretical: Quantum meanderings
I'm interested in quantum information, atomic physics, quantum optics and laser specstrocopy theory. I've been learning these topics in my own time from Paul Berman's Principles of Laser Spectroscopy and Quantum Optics, scientific papers provided by Professor Conover, and other sources. I'm also an avid consumer of linear algebra material, from class (MA253 with Otto Bretscher and MA353 with Leo Livshits) and other sources such as Brilliant.org or quantum mechanics material online (UMD, MIT online lecture notes).

I'm not necessary trying to keep my quantum mechancis self-study tightly structured. I like picking up new concepts as I encounter them. For example, if I'm going to be working on an EIT experiment, I'd try to learn as much as possible about it, both the theoretical and experimental aspects. This unstructured style allows me to explore topics outside what research or class that I might be curious about.

I'm working on an Electromagnetically Induced Transparency (EIT) experiment in the near future. More updates coming soon.

Summer 2018: Ramsey's Separated Oscillating Field Method
I spend most of my summer research at Conover's lab studying how we could use Ramsey's oscillating field method to make high-precision measurements without the need for AC Stark shift elimination. While this was an experimental project, there was not much experimental work involved, as we already had a functioning system for high-precision spectroscopy: (i) Rydberg excitation, (ii) DC-Stark shift elimination, (iii) AC-Stark shift elimination. To run the Ramsey experiment, I simply replaced the previous single-pulse Rydberg excitation with a double-pulse Rydberg excitation (which produced Ramsey fringes), and removed step (iii). It turned out probably 10% of the time was spent on taking data, while the rest went to deriving (from Ramsey's 1950 paper) a model for fitting the data to find the desired (with zero AC-Startk shift) transition frequency.

I presented this work at Colby Undergraduate Summer Research Retreat 2018 (CUSRR 2018). Check out my poster here.
